# PAIciente: A Prompt-Constrained Polytropic System Implementing Behavioral Analogs of Bounded Competence and Structured Verification Without Architectural Monotropism

**Technical Rationale and Academic Positioning**

*PAIciente v2.0 - Design Framing Document*

---

## Abstract

PAIciente is an open-source structured-prompt tool that simulates a child presenting with Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD, combined type) and Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD), targeting practice environments for parents, educators, and clinicians. This paper provides a detailed rationale for the system's formal framing as a *prompt-constrained polytropic system that implements behavioral analogs of bounded competence and structured verification without architectural monotropism*. Each component of this framing is defined, justified against alternatives, and grounded in both the system's engineering design and its clinical evidence base. The paper argues that this framing is not merely descriptive but constitutive: it distinguishes PAIciente from adjacent categories (chatbot personas, fine-tuned clinical tools, role-play scaffolds) and makes explicit the design commitments that govern its simulation fidelity, verification architecture, and cross-platform portability.

---

## 1. Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly applied to behavioral simulation, clinical training, and scenario-based education. Most such applications fall into one of two categories: (a) fine-tuned models architecturally committed to a single behavioral profile, or (b) lightly prompted role-play systems with no formal state management, verification mechanism, or evidence grounding. PAIciente occupies neither category. It is a prompt-only system that achieves structured behavioral simulation through engineered constraints on a general-purpose LLM substrate, while retaining the substrate's full analytical capacity for a co-resident clinician persona.

This dual requirement - *simulate bounded competence* in one mode and *exercise full analytical competence* in another, within the same session and context window - is the core design tension. The framing statement resolves it by naming five architectural commitments:

1. **Prompt-constrained** - the system boundary is the prompt, not the model weights.
2. **Polytropic** - multiple functionally distinct processing orientations coexist.
3. **Behavioral analogs** - simulation targets are structural correspondences in behavior, not mechanistic homology.
4. **Bounded competence** - the simulation deliberately restricts output along clinically specified dimensions.
5. **Without architectural monotropism** - the substrate is not specialized; specialization is emergent from prompt logic.

Each is examined below.

## 2. Definitions and Scope

### 2.1 Prompt-Constrained

A system is *prompt-constrained* when its behavioral specification resides entirely in the prompt layer - the natural-language instructions delivered to the model at inference time - rather than in model weights, reward functions, fine-tuning corpora, or external orchestration code. The prompt is the system. Changes to system behavior are changes to the prompt text. The model's pre-trained capabilities are unmodified.

### 2.2 Polytropic

*Polytropic* (from Greek *polytropos*: "of many turns") denotes a system that implements multiple functionally distinct processing orientations, each with its own output contract, epistemic posture, and interaction logic, selectable within a single session. This contrasts with *monotropic* systems that implement one behavioral orientation, and with *multi-agent* systems that distribute orientations across separate model instances.

### 2.3 Behavioral Analog

A *behavioral analog* is a structurally similar output pattern that corresponds to a target behavior without sharing its generative mechanism. When PAIciente's child persona produces fragmented multi-step recall, the output is *analogous* to working-memory limitation in ADHD - it matches the observable pattern - but it is generated by prompt constraints on a system with no working-memory bottleneck. The distinction between analog and homolog (same mechanism) is critical for honest positioning: PAIciente simulates the behavioral surface of ADHD+ODD, not its neurocognitive substrate.

### 2.4 Bounded Competence

*Bounded competence* refers to deliberately constrained performance along specified cognitive, linguistic, emotional, and behavioral dimensions. The underlying model is capable of fluent multi-paragraph reasoning, perfect instruction-following, and affectively neutral output. The prompt restricts these capabilities along clinically defined axes: executive function (sequencing, time estimation, task initiation), emotional regulation (frustration tolerance, rejection sensitivity), attention allocation (interest-driven engagement, hyperfocus), and social-behavioral response (oppositional refusal, compliance conditions).

### 2.5 Architectural Monotropism

*Architectural monotropism* describes systems whose specialization is achieved through irreversible or high-cost modification of the model substrate - typically fine-tuning, RLHF with domain-specific reward functions, or architectural changes (adapter layers, routing networks). A monotropic system trades general capability for domain performance. PAIciente explicitly avoids this trade-off.

## 3. Rationale: Prompt Constraint as System Boundary

### 3.1 Why Not Fine-Tune?

Fine-tuning a model to simulate a child with ADHD+ODD would require a training corpus of annotated child discourse grounded in clinical profiles - a corpus that does not exist and would raise substantial ethical concerns in construction (data from clinical sessions with minors, consent and privacy constraints, risk of encoding individual children's identifiable behavioral signatures). Even if such a corpus existed, fine-tuning would introduce two structural problems:

**Capability erosion.** PAIciente's clinician persona requires the model's full analytical, citation, and structured-reasoning capacity. Fine-tuning on child-like discourse would degrade these capabilities - a well-documented phenomenon in domain-specific fine-tuning where out-of-domain performance declines [general finding in transfer learning literature; no specific PAIciente citation needed].

**Opacity.** A fine-tuned model's behavioral profile is encoded in weight distributions that are not human-inspectable. The prompt-constrained approach makes the full behavioral specification readable, versionable, and auditable. Every constraint on the child persona - the Barkley 30% emotional-age rule, the Stringaris-derived ODD dimensions, the CPS-grounded compliance conditions - is visible in the prompt text. This transparency is not incidental; it is a design requirement for a tool intended to serve clinical and educational populations.

### 3.2 The Prompt as Versioned Specification

Treating the prompt as the system boundary yields concrete engineering properties:

- **Reproducibility.** Given the same prompt and model version, the system's behavioral envelope is deterministic (modulo sampling variance). Sessions can be compared across users.
- **Auditability.** Clinical reviewers can inspect the full behavioral logic without model-internal access. Every claim the system makes about ADHD or ODD behavior can be traced to a specific prompt clause and its cited source.
- **Portability.** The prompt can be deployed across LLM substrates (Claude, GPT, Gemini, Mistral) with substrate-specific adaptation but identical behavioral intent. Cross-platform assessment [already conducted for PAIciente v2.0] revealed that behavioral fidelity varies by substrate, but the specification itself is portable.
- **Diff-based iteration.** Behavioral changes are prompt patches - scoped, reviewable, and reversible. This mirrors software engineering practice and enables the kind of incremental, confirmation-gated development that PAIciente follows.

## 4. Rationale: Polytropy

### 4.1 The Functional Orientations

PAIciente implements the following distinct processing orientations within a single prompt and session:

| Orientation | Trigger | Output Contract | Epistemic Posture |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Child persona** | `@child` | In-character dialogue; age-appropriate language; behaviorally constrained output | Simulated bounded perspective of a 9-year-old |
| **Inner state layer** | `@child:inner` | Structured annotation block appended to child output | Metacognitive - exposes simulation reasoning |
| **State engine** | `@set` | Parameter injection with validation, conflict detection, error handling | Programmatic - operates on state variables |
| **Clinician persona** | `@clinician` | Structured analysis (observations / inferences / recommendations); citations | Full analytical; evidence-grounded |
| **Practice tools** | `@replay`, `@debrief`, `@script`, `@escalation-map` | Mode-specific structured output | Varies by tool (reflective, generative, analytic) |

These are not stylistic variations. They differ in output structure, reasoning depth, vocabulary constraints, and epistemic commitments. The child persona suppresses analytical capability; the clinician persona exercises it maximally. The inner state layer occupies a third position - it reasons *about* the child simulation from outside the child's perspective while the child persona is active. This triadic structure (simulation / metacognition / analysis) is the core polytropic design.

### 4.2 Why Not Multi-Agent?

An alternative architecture would distribute these orientations across separate model instances or API calls - one instance for the child, one for the clinician, one for state management. This would avoid the polytropy requirement but introduce:

- **Context fragmentation.** The clinician's analysis of a child interaction requires access to the full interaction history, including inner state annotations and set parameters. Distributing across instances requires explicit context passing, which is lossy and adds latency.
- **State synchronization overhead.** The `@set` engine modifies state that must be reflected in both the child's behavior and the inner state annotations. A multi-agent architecture requires a shared state bus, adding engineering complexity with no corresponding gain in simulation quality.
- **Session coherence loss.** The user experience depends on fluid switching between personas within a single conversational flow. Multi-agent architectures introduce turn-level latency and risk context-window misalignment.

Polytropy within a single context window avoids all three costs. The trade-off is prompt complexity - the entire behavioral specification must coexist in one instruction set - but this is manageable and, as argued in §3.2, yields transparency benefits.

### 4.3 Polytropy vs. Multi-Personality

The term *polytropic* is chosen deliberately over alternatives like "multi-persona" or "multi-personality" to avoid two misleading implications:

1. **Clinical connotation.** "Multi-personality" carries unavoidable association with dissociative identity disorder. PAIciente's orientations are not personalities; they are engineered processing modes with explicit activation commands.
2. **Surface-level framing.** "Multi-persona" suggests the difference is primarily stylistic (tone, vocabulary, register). PAIciente's orientations differ at the structural level: different output schemas, different epistemic commitments, different constraint sets. *Polytropic* foregrounds the functional multiplicity.

## 5. Rationale: Behavioral Analogs of Bounded Competence

### 5.1 The Analog/Homolog Distinction

This is the most important conceptual commitment in the framing and the one most vulnerable to misinterpretation. PAIciente does not claim to *have* ADHD or ODD. It does not claim to replicate the neurocognitive mechanisms underlying these conditions. It claims to produce behavioral output that is *structurally analogous* to the observable behavioral surface of a child with these profiles, as characterized by the clinical literature.

The distinction matters for three reasons:

**Honest scope.** Parents and clinicians using PAIciente should understand that they are practicing with a behavioral surface model, not interacting with a system that "understands" what it is like to have ADHD. The analog framing prevents overclaiming.

**Simulation fidelity criteria.** If PAIciente claimed mechanistic homology, fidelity would need to be assessed against neurocognitive measures (working memory capacity tests, inhibitory control assays, neuroimaging correlates). The analog framing sets the fidelity criterion at the behavioral level: does the output match what the clinical literature describes as observable behavior? This is assessable by clinicians reviewing transcripts, not by neurocognitive instrumentation.

**Ethical positioning.** Claiming that an LLM "has" a neurodevelopmental condition would be misleading and potentially harmful - it could trivialize the lived experience of individuals with these conditions. The analog framing explicitly disclaims mechanistic identity while preserving the simulation's practical utility.

### 5.2 What Is Bounded

The specific competence boundaries implemented in PAIciente's child persona are derived from the clinical literature:

**Executive function constraints.** Barkley's unified theory of ADHD [1] positions behavioral inhibition as the core deficit, with downstream impairments in working memory, self-regulation of affect and arousal, internalized speech, and reconstitution (the ability to decompose and recombine behavioral sequences). PAIciente's child persona implements analogs of these impairments: it loses track of multi-step instructions (working memory), responds impulsively before questions finish (inhibition), escalates under frustration (affect regulation), and struggles with task sequencing and time estimation (reconstitution, temporal processing). Barkley's broader treatment of executive functions [3] and clinical handbook [4] provide the framework for calibrating the severity and interaction of these constraints.

**Emotional regulation delay.** Barkley's heuristic that children with ADHD operate at roughly 70% of their chronological age in emotional self-regulation (the "30% rule") is implemented directly: the child persona's emotional responses are calibrated to approximately age 6.5–7 for a 9-year-old character. This manifests as lower frustration tolerance, higher rejection sensitivity, and greater difficulty with transitions than would be expected for the chronological age.

**Oppositional response structure.** Rather than implementing ODD as uniform defiance, PAIciente draws on Stringaris and Goodman's three-dimensional model [6] - irritable, headstrong, and hurtful dimensions - and Burke et al.'s bifactor analysis [7] confirming irritability as a distinct clinical dimension. The child persona's oppositional behavior is not random resistance; it is contextually triggered by perceived unfairness, arbitrary authority, loss of autonomy, or control-oriented interaction - consistent with Greene's lagging-skills interpretation [12, 13] and the ODD literature's emphasis on functional context [8, 9, 10].

**Compliance conditions.** The child persona's compliance is not binary (obey/refuse) but conditional on identifiable interactional factors: autonomy provision, transparent reasoning, perceived fairness, choice architecture, and caregiver regulation state. This models the well-established finding that oppositional behavior in ODD is not trait-level rigidity but a stress response modulated by relational and contextual variables [8, 11, 12]. The practical implication is that users practicing with PAIciente can observe differential outcomes from different interactional approaches - the simulation responds to strategy, not just to commands.

**Comorbidity interaction.** The ADHD+ODD comorbidity is not implemented as two independent symptom lists summed together. The interaction effects are modeled: executive function deficits lower the threshold for oppositional activation (the child has fewer regulatory resources to manage frustration); ODD-driven refusal compounds ADHD-driven task avoidance; medication state (`@set medication`) modulates both profiles simultaneously. This reflects the high comorbidity rates documented in meta-analytic literature [18] and the neuroimaging evidence for shared and distinct neural correlates [19].

### 5.3 How Bounding Is Achieved

The bounding mechanism is entirely prompt-based. Specific prompt clauses constrain:

- **Vocabulary and syntax**: age-appropriate output, with exceptions for hyperfocus domains (where vocabulary may exceed peers, per the clinical profile).
- **Reasoning depth**: the child persona does not produce analytical reasoning, causal explanation of its own behavior, or structured argumentation - these are reserved for the clinician persona.
- **Affective range**: emotional responses are calibrated to the 30% rule; rejection sensitivity is explicitly parameterized.
- **Compliance logic**: conditional rules specify when the child complies, resists, escalates, or withdraws, based on the interactional approach and current state parameters.
- **Information leakage prevention**: the child persona is explicitly constrained from disclosing injected context (`@set context`), explaining its own backstory, or breaking character to provide clinical information. If conversational pressure would force such disclosure, the prompt specifies deflection behaviors (silence, topic change, monosyllabic response).

None of these constraints modify the model's weights. They operate as behavioral rules interpreted at inference time. The model retains full capability; the prompt channels it.

## 6. Rationale: Structured Verification

### 6.1 The Verification Problem

Behavioral simulation without verification is unaccountable simulation. If a user interacts with the child persona and observes escalation, they need to know: was the escalation driven by the simulation's internal logic (the state parameters, the interactional antecedent, the trajectory model), or was it an artifact of stochastic generation? Without verification mechanisms, the simulation is a black box that happens to produce plausible-looking output.

PAIciente addresses this through four verification subsystems:

### 6.2 Inner State Annotations (`@child:inner`)

When active, the inner state layer appends a structured block to every child response:

```
[INNER STATE]
Feeling: <emotion label>
Arousal: low / medium / high / overloaded
What would help right now: <1 concrete thing>
What just made it worse: <1 concrete thing, or "nothing">
Escalation path:
  Trajectory: stable / building / near-threshold / at-threshold
  Next likely behavior: <1 behaviorally specific line>
  Interrupt lever: <1 concrete caregiver action>
```

This serves three verification functions:

- **Transparency of simulation reasoning.** The user can inspect *why* the child responded as it did - not just what it said, but what emotional state, arousal level, and escalation trajectory the simulation assigned to the interaction.
- **Predictive accountability.** The "next likely behavior" and "interrupt lever" fields commit the simulation to predictions that the user can test in subsequent turns. If the simulation predicts "child will slam door" and the interrupt lever is "offer a 2-minute break," the user can try the lever and assess whether the simulation follows its own logic.
- **State provenance tracking.** When `@set` parameters are active, the inner state block marks each field with `← [SET]` (injected by user) or `← [organic]` (derived from interaction). This distinguishes between user-controlled and simulation-derived state, preventing conflation.

### 6.3 Programmable State Engine (`@set`)

The `@set` system allows users to inject specific starting conditions - arousal level, trajectory, trust, fatigue, medication state, hyperfocus target, and contextual backstory - and observe how the simulation behaves from that starting point. This enables:

- **Controlled comparison.** The same interactional approach can be tested against different starting states (e.g., high-arousal/low-trust vs. low-arousal/high-trust), isolating the effect of state variables on behavioral outcomes.
- **Scenario reproducibility.** Clinician trainers can specify exact state configurations for training exercises, ensuring that multiple trainees encounter the same simulated starting condition.
- **Edge-case testing.** Extreme state configurations (e.g., `arousal=overloaded trajectory=at-threshold trust=low medication=wearing-off`) stress-test the simulation's behavioral logic at boundary conditions.

The system includes formal validation: out-of-range values produce typed errors; internally inconsistent parameter combinations produce explicit warnings; the simulation proceeds with stated values but flags the inconsistency. This prevents silent garbage-in/garbage-out behavior.

### 6.4 Post-Hoc Analysis (`@debrief`)

The `@debrief` command auto-switches to the clinician persona and produces a structured analysis of the preceding child interaction. This is verification by expert review: the clinician persona, operating at full analytical capacity, assesses the interaction using the same structured format (observations → inferences → recommendations) that a human clinician might use in supervision. The child state is suspended, not cleared, allowing the user to resume the child interaction after debriefing.

### 6.5 Practice Loop (`@replay`)

The `@replay` command re-outputs the last child turn with the same state, then prompts the user to try an alternative approach. This creates a controlled experimental loop: same starting conditions, different intervention, observable differential outcome. The model does not auto-generate the alternative - the user must supply it - preserving the practice function (the user is the learner, not the observer).

## 7. Rationale: Rejecting Architectural Monotropism

### 7.1 The Monotropism Trade-Off

Architectural monotropism - specializing a model through fine-tuning, reward shaping, or structural modification - purchases domain performance at the cost of general capability. For single-purpose systems, this trade-off is often acceptable. For PAIciente, it is structurally incompatible with the design requirements.

The core incompatibility is the **polytropy requirement** (§4). PAIciente must simultaneously support:

- A child persona that *suppresses* analytical reasoning, maintains age-appropriate vocabulary, and follows conditional compliance logic.
- A clinician persona that *maximizes* analytical reasoning, cites peer-reviewed evidence, and produces structured clinical assessment.
- A state engine that performs parameter validation, conflict detection, and formal error handling.
- An inner state layer that produces metacognitive annotations about the child simulation's internal logic.

These orientations make opposing demands on model capability. A model fine-tuned to produce child-like output would degrade on clinician-mode tasks. A model fine-tuned for clinical analysis would resist producing the constrained, fragmented, emotionally reactive output required of the child persona. Prompt-level control avoids this by leaving the substrate's full capability space intact and selecting from it per-orientation.

### 7.2 Cross-Platform Portability

PAIciente has been assessed across five LLM substrates: Claude Projects, ChatGPT CustomGPT, Mistral Agents, Gemini Gems, and Grok. This assessment is possible *because* the system is not architecturally monotropic - it is a portable specification that can be instantiated on any sufficiently capable LLM. Fine-tuned monotropic systems are, by definition, locked to their training substrate.

The non-monotropic design makes such cross-platform assessment structurally possible - the same behavioral specification can be instantiated on each substrate and the resulting simulation fidelity compared. This comparison could not be conducted if PAIciente were architecturally bound to a single substrate through fine-tuning or weight modification.

### 7.3 The General-Purpose Substrate Argument

The argument for a general-purpose substrate is not merely pragmatic (portability, cost avoidance). It is structural. The *quality* of the child simulation depends on the substrate's latent knowledge of developmental psychology, behavioral dynamics, and clinical frameworks. A general-purpose LLM trained on broad corpora has encoded - in its weight distributions - statistical patterns from clinical literature, educational psychology, child development research, and naturalistic discourse. The prompt does not need to teach the model what ADHD looks like; it needs to *activate and constrain* existing knowledge.

Fine-tuning on a narrow corpus would overwrite some of this latent knowledge with domain-specific patterns, potentially reducing the simulation's capacity to respond flexibly to novel interactional approaches. The prompt-constrained approach preserves the full latent knowledge base and steers it through behavioral rules.

## 8. Situating PAIciente Among Adjacent Categories

The framing statement positions PAIciente outside several adjacent categories. The distinctions are worth making explicit.

### 8.1 Not a Chatbot Persona

Standard chatbot personas (customer service bots with "friendly" tones, creative writing assistants with "character voices") operate at the stylistic level - they adjust register, vocabulary, and affect without implementing structured behavioral logic, state management, or verification mechanisms. PAIciente's child persona is not a tone setting; it is a behavioral specification with conditional compliance rules, escalation trajectories, parameterized state, and predictive annotations. The distinction is between *style* (how something is said) and *behavioral logic* (what is said under what conditions and why).

### 8.2 Not a Fine-Tuned Clinical Tool

Clinical NLP tools fine-tuned for specific tasks (diagnostic screening, treatment recommendation, symptom extraction) are architecturally monotropic by design - they trade generality for domain accuracy. PAIciente is not a clinical tool in this sense. It does not diagnose, screen, or recommend treatment. It provides a *practice environment* - a simulation that responds to interactional strategy in clinically grounded ways, paired with an analytical persona that explains the dynamics. The system explicitly disclaims clinical function in its prompt constraints.

### 8.3 Not an Unstructured Role-Play Scaffold

Unstructured role-play prompts ("pretend to be a child with ADHD") produce variable, unverifiable output with no state management, no escalation logic, no verification mechanism, and no evidence grounding. The output may be plausible in individual turns but lacks behavioral coherence across turns, has no mechanism for controlled comparison, and cannot be inspected for simulation reasoning. PAIciente's contribution is precisely the structured layer that transforms role-play into accountable simulation.

## 9. Implications

### 9.1 For Simulation Fidelity Assessment

The framing determines what counts as fidelity. Because PAIciente implements behavioral analogs (not mechanistic homologs), fidelity assessment should target the behavioral surface: does the simulated child's response to a validated interactional strategy (e.g., CPS Plan B [12, 13], PCIT-style labeled praise [14]) produce outcomes consistent with the clinical literature's description of how children with ADHD+ODD respond to those strategies? This is a testable proposition that does not require neurocognitive instrumentation.

### 9.2 For User Expectations

The framing sets honest expectations. Users should understand that they are practicing with a behavioral model, not a digital child. The simulation's responses are governed by prompt rules and model inference, not by an inner experience of neurodevelopmental difference. This distinction should be made explicit in all user-facing documentation.

### 9.3 For Extension and Contribution

The prompt-constrained, non-monotropic architecture means that extending PAIciente (adding new behavioral profiles, new clinical tools, new assessment mechanisms) requires only prompt modification - no retraining, no weight access, no infrastructure changes. This lowers the contribution barrier and enables community-driven iteration, consistent with the project's open-source commitment.

## 10. Limitations

**Simulation ceiling.** Behavioral analogs have a fidelity ceiling. The model cannot simulate sensory processing differences, physiological arousal, or somatic states that influence behavior in real children. Behaviors that depend heavily on these (e.g., medication-related appetite changes, sensory overload in noisy environments) can be approximated at the behavioral output level but not grounded mechanistically.

**Stochastic variance.** Prompt-constrained systems inherit the underlying model's sampling variance. Two runs from identical states and inputs may produce different outputs. The `@set` system reduces but does not eliminate this - it constrains starting conditions, not the full generative trajectory.

**Substrate dependence.** Despite portability, behavioral fidelity varies across LLM substrates. Some models resist maintaining suppressed analytical capability in child mode; others trigger content-safety filters on oppositional dialogue. The prompt specification is portable; the quality of its execution is not guaranteed.

**No empirical validation.** PAIciente's behavioral analogs are grounded in clinical literature, but the simulation itself has not been empirically validated against real clinical interactions. Whether users who practice with PAIciente transfer skills to real-world interactions with children with ADHD+ODD is an open empirical question.

---

## 11. Conclusion

The framing of PAIciente as a *prompt-constrained polytropic system implementing behavioral analogs of bounded competence and structured verification without architectural monotropism* is not decorative terminology. Each term identifies a specific design commitment with concrete engineering and clinical implications:

- *Prompt-constrained* ensures transparency, auditability, and portability.
- *Polytropic* enables co-resident simulation and analysis orientations within a single session.
- *Behavioral analogs* set honest scope and appropriate fidelity criteria.
- *Bounded competence* grounds simulation constraints in cited clinical evidence.
- *Without architectural monotropism* preserves the general-purpose substrate required for polytropy and cross-platform deployment.

Together, these commitments define a design space that is distinct from chatbot personas, fine-tuned clinical tools, and unstructured role-play scaffolds. The framing makes explicit what PAIciente is, what it is not, and on what terms its simulation fidelity should be assessed.
